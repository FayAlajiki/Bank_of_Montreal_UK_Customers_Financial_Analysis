# Bank of Montreal Customer Segmentation Analysis

[View Visualization] (https://public.tableau.com/app/profile/fay.alajiki/viz/BMOCustomers2015/Dashboard1)

**Executive Summary**

This project analyzed customer demographic and financial data for Bank of Montreal (BMO) customers in the United Kingdom in 2015. The objective was to understand how customer balances varied across age groups, gender, occupation type, and region, and to generate insights that could support customer segmentation, targeted marketing, and product strategy.

Using Excel for data cleaning and preparation and Tableau for dashboard development, I created an interactive dashboard to highlight patterns in customer concentration and balance distribution. The analysis showed that England had the highest customer concentration with 2,159 customers, while customers aged 30 represented the largest age segment at 40%. It also showed that the customer base was slightly male-dominated at 54%, and that white-collar customers formed the largest occupational segment at 49%.

These findings suggest an opportunity for BMO to focus more strongly on working-age professionals in high-concentration regions while also identifying ways to grow engagement in lower-representation markets.

**Business Problem**

Banks need a clear understanding of their customer base in order to design relevant products, improve targeting, and strengthen customer engagement. Without a structured view of demographic and financial patterns, it becomes more difficult to identify valuable segments, personalize product offerings, and allocate marketing resources effectively.

This project addressed that challenge by examining customer distribution across region, age, gender, occupation, and account balance.

**Methodology**

I began by cleaning and preparing the dataset in Excel to ensure consistency and accuracy across the variables used for analysis. After preparing the data, I used Tableau to build an interactive dashboard that allowed stakeholders to explore customer segments across multiple dimensions.

The dashboard was designed to answer key business questions, including which regions had the highest customer concentration, what age groups made up the largest share of customers, how the customer base was distributed by gender, which job divisions were most represented, and how customers were spread across balance brackets.

**Skills**

**Results and Business Recommendations**

The analysis found that England had the largest customer concentration with 2,159 customers, followed by Scotland with 1,124, Wales with 520, and Northern Ireland with 211. This showed that England represented BMO’s strongest customer base within the dataset and would likely be the highest-impact region for targeted campaigns.

Age analysis showed that customers aged 30 accounted for 40% of the customer base, followed by age 40 at 27% and age 20 at 17%. This indicated that the customer base was primarily made up of working-age adults, which is valuable for financial product targeting and campaign planning.

Gender distribution was fairly balanced, with 54% male and 46% female customers, suggesting that segmentation opportunities were likely more meaningful across age, occupation, and region than across gender alone.

From an occupational perspective, white-collar customers made up 49% of the customer base, compared with 26% blue-collar and 25% other occupations. This suggested that BMO could benefit from tailoring product messaging and offerings toward the needs of professional working customers.

Balance analysis showed that most customers were concentrated in the lower to mid balance brackets, especially in the 0K, 16K, and 32K ranges, while very few customers appeared in the highest balance categories. This indicated stronger representation among moderate-balance account holders than high-balance customers.

Based on these findings, the recommendations were to focus marketing and customer engagement efforts on working-age professionals, especially those in the 30 to 40 age range, prioritize campaigns and product strategies in England where customer concentration was strongest, develop more tailored offerings for white-collar customers, explore growth opportunities in Wales and Northern Ireland, and design retention strategies for moderate-balance customers who made up the largest share of the portfolio.

**Next Steps**

Possible next steps for this analysis would include comparing segmentation patterns across multiple years to identify customer trends over time, analyzing customer behavior by product type or account activity, assessing whether balance levels vary significantly within occupation or regional groups, and developing customer personas for more targeted marketing and product development.
